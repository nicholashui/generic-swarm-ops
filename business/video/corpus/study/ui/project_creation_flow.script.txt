Let's talk about how projects work, and why they sit above the actual production runs. The whole idea here is to give you a layer where you can plan, iterate, and collaborate with your team before any agents ever spin up, and before a single dollar gets spent.

Start with the core distinction. A project is a container. It's free to create, it lives on persistently, and it's built for collaboration. A production, on the other hand, is an execution. That's the part that actually costs money, runs the agents, and produces real artifacts.

Picture a project called "Brand Campaign Q3." Creating it is free, holding onto it is free, and planning inside it is free. Inside that project you might keep shared assets like your brand kit, your voices, and style references. You've got a team, made up of an owner plus editors and reviewers. There's a budget pool, say two hundred and forty dollars set aside. And there are default settings for things like compliance, models, and platforms. Then come the productions themselves. One might be a finished hero video that cost sixty-two dollars. Another could be a TikTok cut that's currently running and has spent twenty-eight dollars so far. And a third might be a training piece still sitting as a draft, costing nothing yet.

Here's the rule that ties it all together. No money is spent until you explicitly hit launch. Everything that happens before that moment is completely free preparation.

So what does the journey look like, from nothing all the way to a running production? First you create a project, which is instant and free. Then you prepare, gathering assets, inviting your team, and choosing settings, and that can take days or even weeks, still at no cost. Next you create a production draft, where your brief is saved and stays editable, again free. After that you get a cost estimate, a preview from the planner, which is also free. Then comes the launch, and that's the moment money starts flowing and the agents begin. From there you monitor progress and approve along the way while the production runs. Finally it's delivered and complete. And when you're ready for the next one, you simply repeat from the draft step.

Now let's walk through the actual screens. The dashboard is where you land. At the top sits a section called "My Projects," with a button to create a new project. Each project shows up as a card. Brand Q3 might show three productions, with two running and ninety dollars spent, last updated a couple of minutes ago. Next to it, a project called Luna Short Film shows one completed production at ninety-five dollars, last touched three days ago. And there's always a card to start a fresh project.

Below that you'll find active productions gathered from across all of your projects, so you can see everything in motion at a glance, each one with a little progress bar. And then there's a quick start area, for when you just want to make something fast and skip the setup. You pick a template, and the system handles the project for you behind the scenes. The templates cover things like hooks, user-generated content, explainers, birthday videos, films, and more.

When you do create a new project, a dialog opens up. You give it a name. You can add an optional description, something like a Q3 brand awareness campaign spanning social and web. Then there's the budget pool, where you set a total budget for all the productions inside, or you can choose no limit and simply pay as you go. You pick a billing method too, like a credit card on file.

The team section is optional and you can always add people later. You're the owner by default, and you can invite others by email and assign a role. The roles are worth understanding. An owner has full control, including billing and the ability to delete. An editor can create and launch productions and manage assets. A reviewer can view, comment, and approve gates. And a viewer gets read-only access.

There's also a defaults section that applies to every production unless you override it. You can set compliance options, like C2PA, accessibility standards, performer agreements, or privacy regulations. You choose a model preference, whether you want cost-optimized, quality-first, or speed-first. And you can connect a brand kit now or add one later. A friendly reminder sits at the bottom: creating a project is free, and you're only charged when you launch a production.

Once you're inside a project, you reach the project workspace. At the top is the project name, with options to archive it or open its settings. There's a row of tabs: overview, productions, assets, team, settings, and activity.

On the overview tab you get a set of status cards at a glance. One shows your productions, maybe three of them, one complete, one running, one in draft. Another shows the budget, ninety dollars out of two hundred and forty, about thirty-eight percent used. A third shows the team, perhaps three members. And a fourth shows your assets, say twelve files.

Below the cards is the productions list itself, with a button to start a new one, plus sorting and filtering. Each production is laid out clearly. The hero video is marked complete, a Type E piece finished two days ago, costing sixty-two dollars, running five minutes and twenty seconds, and delivered to YouTube and TikTok, with options to view its artifacts, check analytics, or duplicate it as a draft. The TikTok cut is running, a Type A piece in its production phase, twenty-eight dollars in, fifty-five percent done, with about two minutes to go, and you can open its console or pause it. The training module is still a draft, a Type F piece that hasn't launched, costing nothing, with its brief eighty percent complete and an estimated cost of around thirty-five dollars, offering buttons to edit the brief, get an estimate, launch, or delete.

Underneath all that is a recent activity feed. It might note that a couple of minutes ago the editor agent finished a rough cut on the TikTok piece, that five minutes ago you approved its first gate, that two days ago the hero video was delivered to YouTube and TikTok, and that three days ago Sarah joined as a reviewer.

The assets tab is your shared library. Up top are buttons to upload assets or connect a brand kit. The brand kit section holds your logo, fonts, color definitions, a guide, and a tone document, and all of it is automatically loaded into every production's brand agent. Then there's a voice library. You might have a brand voice that's energetic and young, cloned through a voice service. Another that's authoritative and mature, custom trained. And a neutral, clear narrator using standard text-to-speech. You can add more voices anytime.

There's a style references section, holding things like a mood image described as neo-noir, a pacing reference video, and a framing reference. And finally a scripts and documents area, with files like a brand messaging guide, target audience research, and a competitor analysis. The key point here is that everything in this library is automatically available to every production in the project, and the agents reference it without anyone needing to re-upload.

Next is the production draft screen, where you edit everything before launching. At the heart of it is the brief editor. You see the chosen template, in this case corporate training, which you can change. You give the production a title, like "Training Module: Product Onboarding." Then there's the vision, a written description, for example a ten-minute interactive training video that teaches new employees how to use the product dashboard, something that should feel modern and friendly and include knowledge checks.

From there you set the details. Duration of ten minutes, a widescreen format, a tone that's friendly and professional. You list learning objectives, like navigating the dashboard and creating a report. You choose an assessment type, perhaps a quiz after each section. And you pick delivery formats, like the common e-learning packaging standards or a standalone video. You can pull in references from the project, such as the brand kit and a specific brand voice, and you can add extras of your own, like a zip of product screenshots. You set a budget for this production, say thirty-five dollars, and you can see the project pool has a hundred and fifty of its two hundred and forty remaining. Everything auto-saves, so it'll note that it last saved a couple of minutes ago.

Then there's the cost estimate preview. You can refresh it anytime, and it shows when it was last calculated. It breaks the cost down piece by piece: a few dollars for script and instructional design, around twelve for rendering the ten-minute avatar, four for voice synthesis, six for motion graphics, a couple for generating the assessment, three for quality control and compliance, and one for packaging it for a learning system. That adds up to a total estimate of around thirty-one dollars, give or take fifteen percent. It also tells you how many agents are needed, around eighteen, and the estimated duration, roughly eight minutes.

There's a section for team comments on the draft. Sarah, a reviewer, might ask whether you can add a section on report exports, and you reply that it's a good idea and you've added it to the learning objectives. Anyone can chime in.

And then the actions. You can save the draft, share it for review, or launch the production. The launch button is upfront about what it will do. It will charge up to thirty-five dollars from the project budget pool. It will activate eighteen agents, covering instructional design, avatar, voice, the learning system, and more. It begins the production, and you'll monitor it in the production console. And it estimates completion at about eight minutes.

When you hit launch, a confirmation dialog appears so there are no surprises. It names the production, the template, and the project. It gives a cost summary: an estimated thirty-one dollars against a budget cap of thirty-five, a remaining project budget of a hundred and fifty, and roughly a hundred and nineteen left after this launch. It spells out what will happen: the planner decomposes your brief, eighteen agents activate, you'll watch progress on the console, gate approvals will pause for your review, and completion is estimated at about eight minutes. You check a box confirming you understand this will charge your project budget, and then you launch.

Stepping back, here's how the overall information architecture fits together. At the root is the dashboard, which holds your projects grid, the active productions across everything, and quick start. Then there's the project workspace, which is new, holding the overview, the productions list, the shared assets, the team, the settings, and the activity log. There's the production draft, also new, holding the brief editor, the cost estimate, team comments, and the launch button. After launch you move into the production console, which already existed, with its canvas of the agent graph, a timeline view, an artifact gallery, a critique feed, gate controls, and an agent inspector. And around all of that sit the existing areas: the agent registry, memory and knowledge, the delivery hub, settings and admin, and help and docs.

On the backend, the project layer is worth noting because no agents are involved at all. It's pure create, read, update, and delete operations. You can create and list projects, fetch or update a single one, and archive it. You can upload, list, and remove shared assets. You can invite members, change their roles, and remove them. And for productions, you can create a draft for free, list them, edit a brief while it's still a draft, preview a cost estimate, and then launch, which is the one and only point where agents actually start. You can also pause a running production, resume a paused one, duplicate a production as a new draft, or delete a draft.

It's worth understanding the shape of the data too, at least conceptually. A project carries an identity and a name, an optional description, an owner, and a status that's either active or archived. It tracks its budget pool, which can be null meaning no limit, along with how much has been spent and the billing method. It holds its members, each with a user, a role, and the date they joined. It holds shared assets, each typed as a brand kit, a voice, a style reference, a document, or a script, with a name, a location, and some metadata. It carries default settings for compliance, model strategy, and platform targets. And it records when it was created and last updated.

A production, in turn, belongs to a project. It has a title and a template, identified by a letter from A through J. It moves through a lifecycle of statuses: draft, launching, running, paused, completed, or failed. It holds a brief, which stays editable while it's a draft, capturing the vision, an optional genre, the duration, the aspect ratio, the tone, the target audience, references by asset, any constraints, the platform targets, and template-specific details like learning objectives or beat counts. It tracks a budget cap and the amount spent. It can hold an estimate from the planner preview, including low, mid, and high cost figures, the number of agents needed, an estimated duration, a breakdown by category, and when it was generated. It keeps team comments on the draft. And only after launch does it gain things like the agent graph, the artifacts, and the timestamps for when it launched and completed.

Finally, for impatient users, there's a quick start path that still moves fast. From the dashboard you click a template, say a viral hook. The system automatically creates an untitled project for you, which you can rename later, and a production with a pre-filled brief, and it drops you right onto the brief editor in draft mode. You fill in the minimal info, hit launch, and the agents start. The project still exists quietly behind the scenes, so you can add more productions later, rename it, and invite a team, but the initial friction is almost nothing.

So to sum up what really changed with this project layer. Before, the dashboard led straight into a brief studio and an immediate launch. Now it flows through a project, a draft, an editing phase, and a launch only when you're ready. Before, every production stood alone. Now productions live inside projects. Before, assets were uploaded for each production separately. Now there's a shared asset library for the whole project. Before, there was no team collaboration before launch. Now your team reviews drafts and leaves comments. Before, you had no visibility into cost before committing. Now you get a free estimate on any draft. Before, you couldn't iterate on a brief without spending. Now draft mode means unlimited free edits. Before, there was no way to group related videos. Now a project groups all the variants of a campaign together. And before, budgets were per-production only. Now there's a budget pool across the whole project alongside per-production caps.
