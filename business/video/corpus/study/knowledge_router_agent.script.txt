Let's talk about something called the Knowledge Router Agent. Think of it as the brain that sits in the middle of a whole system of specialized AI agents, making sure each one gets exactly the knowledge it needs, right when it needs it. This is version one of the design, and it's meant to be production ready. It's been rethought from the ground up, drawing on research papers, on real-world best practices, and on a very specific set of use cases.

So what world does this thing live in? Two big ones, really. The first is AI filmmaking. That means turning text into video, keeping characters and scenes consistent, and building cinematic pipelines that actually hold together. The second is AI agents, which covers orchestrating lots of agents at once, doing advanced retrieval, and building systems that improve themselves over time. The guiding philosophy behind all of it is simple to state and hard to do well. Be specification driven. Lean heavily on critic loops, where the system checks its own work. Blend fixed, rule-based routing with routing that's learned from experience. Make everything traceable. And keep getting better.

Here's the big picture. Imagine you have a growing library of around five thousand markdown files, full of techniques, notes, and hard-won lessons. Every specialized agent in your system, whether it's a character consistency critic, a video prompt optimizer, a multi-agent orchestrator designer, or a shot planning agent, needs to pull from that library. But it needs the right material, with as little noise as possible, with high precision, and with a clear explanation of why those pieces were chosen. That's the entire job of the Knowledge Router. It's the smart gateway between any agent that's asking and the curated knowledge underneath.

This design pulls ideas from the cutting edge of recent research. There's graph-guided routing that uses a neural network supervised by actual performance. There's centroid-based retrieval combined with iterative planning. There are reflection tokens, where the system tags its own thinking. There's corrective retrieval that fixes itself when results look weak. And there are production patterns borrowed from real multi-agent systems in the wild. But the key thing is, while it borrows from all of these, it stays general enough to work for any knowledge-heavy domain.

Let me walk through the core innovations, because they're what make this design distinctive. First, there's a hybrid routing stack. It works in layers. It starts with metadata, then moves to semantic clustering around representative central points, then explores a graph of relationships, and finally uses a language model to rank and reflect. Second, there's a combined planner and router for complex pipelines that need multiple hops to answer. Third, there's a built-in critic that works on several levels at once, judging retrieval quality, the routing decision itself, and how useful the result turned out to be downstream. Fourth, the whole thing improves based on performance, learning from whether the agents it served actually succeeded. Fifth, it's traceable and explainable by design, not as an afterthought. And sixth, it can bootstrap without any training at all, with the option to add learned components later. It even ships with domain packs tailored to key agents.

Now let's get into purpose and success. The purpose is to be the single intelligent gateway between any requesting agent and the knowledge base. It has to understand who is asking, what they need, and why. Then it delivers the best possible context, along with the reasoning behind it.

How do we know it's working? There are clear quality gates. Retrieval precision, meaning the relevant files actually show up near the top, should be at least eighty-eight percent. Routing decisions should measurably lift how well the downstream agents perform. Speed matters too, so standard queries should finish in under four seconds for the vast majority of cases, and complex pipeline queries under eight seconds. Every single decision must produce an explanation that a human can read and that's also structured for machines. The system's accuracy should improve over time as the critic feeds back lessons. And it should be cost efficient, avoiding the trap of grabbing far more than it needs.

Let's look at the architecture. A requesting agent, say the character consistency critic, sends in a structured request. That request flows into the Knowledge Router, which runs through a series of stages. First, a query analyzer figures out the intent, with a moment of reflection built in. Then, for complex multi-step queries, a planner kicks in. After that comes the heart of the system, the hybrid retrieval engine. Inside that engine, there are four sub-stages. It applies fast, deterministic metadata filters. It does semantic retrieval around cluster centers. It explores a knowledge graph, either with lightweight traversal or with a small trained network. And it uses a language model to rank candidates and reflect on them. Once retrieval is done, a context assembler packages everything up, an explainer writes out the full reasoning, and the multi-level critic evaluates the whole thing. Underneath it all sits the knowledge base itself: thousands of markdown files with rich structured headers, a vector store for embeddings, a knowledge graph connecting techniques, tools, failure modes, papers, and agents, and optionally some precomputed centers for each domain or agent cluster.

A few design principles are worth emphasizing. Metadata comes first, because hard constraints can instantly throw out seventy to eighty percent of irrelevant files. The graph gives the system genuine intelligence about relationships, so it understands that, for example, character consistency techniques can improve multi-shot storytelling in a given tool. For complex queries, it refines iteratively, planning and then evaluating and simplifying. It reflects on its own retrieval quality before committing to an answer. And while the core logic stays domain-agnostic, the relevance tags and domain packs make it especially powerful for a corpus that mixes filmmaking and agent engineering.

Now, the input and output contract is strict and rich, and that's deliberate. When an agent makes a request, it sends a unique identifier, a timestamp, its own name, and a plain description of the task. For example, ensuring that a character's face, clothing, and props stay consistent across twelve shots in a cinematic martial-arts fight scene, using a hybrid workflow of two specific video tools. It lists the concepts it needs, like character consistency, multi-shot work, reference sheets, and known failure modes such as clothing drift. It sets constraints, like a maximum number of files, a token budget, a preference for recent material, a minimum quality score, and tags to exclude. It supplies context, including previous failures it ran into, like faces morphing in certain shots or colors shifting under dramatic lighting, plus the desired style and the downstream goal. And finally it gives routing hints, such as how complex the query is, whether the graph is needed, and whether multiple hops are expected.

The output is just as detailed. The router returns the selected knowledge as a list of files, each one with a title, a relevance score, a clear reason for the match, key excerpts pulled from the file, the tags that matched, and which agents the file is relevant to. Then there's a context pack, which can be a structured bundle with sections for best practices, failure modes and how to mitigate them, and tool-specific notes. After that comes a reasoning trace that spells out each step. For instance, it might say that hard metadata filters narrowed five thousand files down to forty-seven candidates, that semantic clustering surfaced the most relevant groups, that graph traversal pulled in a few related technique files by following relationships, and that the language model ranker scored a dozen files, flagged two as only partly relevant, and settled on nine. It even explains why it chose those over the alternatives, often prioritizing files that directly cover the failures mentioned in the request. The output also includes the critic's evaluation, with scores for retrieval quality and routing confidence, a judgment on expected usefulness, and concrete suggestions for improvement. And it closes with suggested next actions, like requesting deeper exploration on a related concept or flagging a file for quality review.

Let's go deeper into the core components. The query analyzer parses the task description, the required concepts, and the previous failures. It pulls out entities, like which tools and which concepts are involved. It classifies how complex the request is and decides whether the planner is needed. And it uses a light touch of reflection, asking itself whether this is about a single technique or a full pipeline.

The planner activates for high-complexity or pipeline queries. When the hints say the query is a pipeline, or that multiple hops are expected, the planner breaks the work into sub-tasks. For a video project, that might be creating a character bible, then defining shot-by-shot consistency rules, then working out lighting-specific fixes, then nailing down tool-specific prompt patterns. It routes these sub-tasks either one after another or in parallel, and it runs a loop that simplifies questions and evaluates responses, capped at four or five rounds so it doesn't spin forever.

The hybrid retrieval engine is the heart, and it works in a strict order. First, metadata hard filters do fast, deterministic matching on things like agent relevance, category, tag overlap, quality score, and recency, cutting the field by seventy to eighty percent. Second, a centroid-based semantic retriever, inspired by efficient clustering approaches, precomputes coherent clusters for each major subcategory and stores only the central points and representative files. A query turns into an embedding, finds the top clusters, and then expands into the files within them. This is what lets the system scale without broadcasting every query across all five thousand files. Third, a graph explorer traverses relationships between nodes representing techniques, tools, failure modes, papers, and agent roles, connected by relations like improves, requires, commonly fails when, used together with, and mitigates. A typical path might start at character consistency, follow an improves link to multi-shot narrative, and branch to a failure mode about clothing drift under dramatic lighting. Fourth and finally, a language model ranker with reflection scores the candidates against the task, the previous failures, and the constraints. It pauses to ask whether these passages are truly useful for the downstream goal and whether anything critical is missing, and if quality looks low it can trigger a corrective re-retrieval.

The context assembler decides how to package the result. It might return raw top chunks, a synthesized summary, or a structured knowledge pack, which tends to be best for critic-heavy agents. For creative agents, it usually returns a knowledge pack with clear sections: best practices, failure modes and mitigations, tool-specific notes, and prompt templates.

The explainer and traceability layer guarantees that every output includes a clear reasoning trace, both structured and in natural language. This is non-negotiable, because it's what makes debugging and the critic loops possible.

The multi-level critic is the self-improving core, and it works on three levels. The retrieval critic scores relevance, how well the required concepts are covered, and how well the previous failures are handled. The routing critic judges whether the right files were chosen versus the alternatives, and it suggests better tags or new graph connections. And the downstream utility critic, in the ideal case, gets feedback from the requesting agent after it actually uses the knowledge, asking whether it helped and what was missing. That feedback becomes a soft supervision signal, much like training targets based on real success. Over time, this turns the router into a genuine learning system.

Now let's see how this plays out in particular use cases. For the character consistency critic, take that martial-arts fight scene. The router applies a hard filter for files tagged with character consistency and multi-shot. The graph pulls in clothing-drift failure nodes and their mitigation techniques. It returns a structured pack covering reference sheet practices, lighting-induced color shift fixes, and a multi-shot coherence checklist. And the critic double-checks that the previous failures are actually covered.

For the video prompt optimizer, the router prioritizes files about prompt engineering, camera movement, lighting, negative prompts, and proven prompt formulas. It uses graph traversal to find a formula that worked with a dynamic camera in cinematic scenes, and it returns prompt templates, before-and-after examples, and common failure prompts to avoid.

For the multi-agent orchestrator designer, the router heads to files about multi-agent patterns, orchestration frameworks, role definitions, memory architectures, and production-ready patterns. The planner breaks the problem into orchestrating consistency across shots and optimizing cost and latency for video generation agents. It returns role definitions, graph patterns, and real pipeline examples from the corpus.

For the shot planning agent, which goes from script to shot list to generation, the high complexity activates the planner. It routes iteratively across scriptwriting, the language of cinematography, tool-specific shot capabilities, and consistency constraints. The final pack is a structured shot list template with per-shot prompting guidance and consistency guardrails.

And here's the nice part about generalizing. For any new domain, you just add the relevant tags in the file headers, define new clusters and graph node types, and optionally create a small domain pack template. That's it.

That brings us to how the knowledge base needs to be set up. Every markdown file must carry rich structured frontmatter, and this is non-negotiable for the router to work well. That means a title, a category like AI filmmaking or AI agents or their intersection, a subcategory, a set of tags, a list of which agents the file is relevant to, a domain label, a quality score rated by a human or a critic, the source type, and dates for when it was added and last reviewed. For scaling, it also helps to precompute a cluster or centroid identifier per file and to add graph node references.

The implementation is phased. Phase one is the minimum viable product, taking roughly one to two weeks. That's metadata filters plus basic vector search, a simple query analyzer, a basic assembler and explainer, a lightweight manual critic loop, and bootstrapping with your existing top five hundred to a thousand highest-quality files. Phase two is the production core, which adds the centroid clustering layer, a lightweight graph explorer starting with traversal, the planner for pipeline queries, structured knowledge pack output, and automated critics at the first two levels. Phase three turns it into a learning system, with a performance feedback loop where downstream success becomes soft labels, an optional small trained router network, proactive suggestions, and full self-improvement through critic feedback and usage analytics. Phase four is the advanced stage: cost and latency aware routing, multi-modal support if you add image and video references, and full integration with the main critic agent and the broader system harness.

For evaluation, there are several angles. Offline, you build a golden test set of fifty to a hundred representative queries per major agent role and measure precision, recall of required concepts, and critic scores. Online, you track how downstream agent success changes before and after router improvements, logging critic scores and doing human spot checks. With ablation, you test the impact of each layer, comparing metadata alone against adding the graph, against adding reflection. And continuously, the router's own critic proposes improvements to the knowledge base itself, suggesting new tags or detecting missing content.

There are edge cases too, and each has a mitigation. A very broad query forces the planner to decompose it and apply strict limits on the number of files. When there are no good matches, the router honestly says the knowledge is insufficient, suggests expanding the corpus, and flags low confidence. When files conflict with each other, the graph and critic prioritize higher quality scores, more recent material, and explicit failure mode coverage. When a brand-new agent role appears, you simply add it to the relevance tags, and the router gradually learns through feedback. And when latency spikes on complex queries, the planner's round limits kick in and it falls back to simpler retrieval.

On the technology side, the recommendations align with a typical modern stack. For orchestration, use a stateful graph-based framework or a custom harness that handles the planner, router, and critic loops well. For vectors and metadata, use a strong indexing library paired with a vector database that filters well on metadata. For the graph, start with a lightweight library for traversal and add a small trained network later if you want. Use a high-quality embedding model. For the analyzer, ranker, and critic, mix a strong reasoning model with cheaper models for the simple steps. And store all the markdown files in version control, alongside the vector database and, optionally, a graph database.

Finally, about generalizing to any domain: this design is deliberately domain-agnostic at its core. You replace the category, subcategory, and relevance taxonomy with your own. You define new clusters and graph schemas. And the pattern of hybrid retrieval plus planner plus multi-level critic transfers directly. In fact, a corpus that mixes filmmaking and agent knowledge is an excellent stress test, because it combines creative, technical, and meta-agent material. If the router succeeds there, it will generalize extremely well.

So where do you go from here? The recommended path is to review and refine the specification with your main critic agent, start the phase one build, audit and enrich the frontmatter on your most valuable files, and then build the first version of the metadata and vector layer, testing it with two or three of your real agents. This whole specification has been deeply researched and rethought for production robustness, for these specific creative and agent use cases, and for alignment with the latest research patterns. It's ready to implement, and ready to keep evolving until it fits perfectly into your ecosystem.
