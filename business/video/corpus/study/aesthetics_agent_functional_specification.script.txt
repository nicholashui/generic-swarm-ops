Imagine you're building a sprawling system made up of more than a hundred specialized AI agents, all working together to produce video. Each one is good at its narrow job, but none of them really has an eye. None of them can look at a frame and say, that's beautiful, or that's off, or that misses the mood we were going for. This is the story of the one agent designed to give the whole swarm that eye. It's called the Aesthetics Agent, and what we're walking through is its complete functional specification, a production-grade blueprint meant to be handed straight to engineers and built.

Let's start with the big idea. The Aesthetics Agent is the swarm's computational version of what artists call artiste sense, that intuitive feel for composition, color, light, and emotion. The key thing to understand is that it doesn't replace human taste. Instead, it amplifies, encodes, and spreads human taste at superhuman speed across all hundred and fourteen agents. Think of it as a shared aesthetic nervous system.

It does three distinct jobs that fit together. First, it's the Critic. It perceives. It looks at any visual artifact, a single frame, a still image, or a full video clip, and scores it against a detailed rubric covering things like composition, color harmony, lighting, depth, emotional resonance, technical quality, style fidelity, and novelty. This is the swarm's shared eye. Second, it's the Aligner. It refines. It takes the Critic's judgments and turns them into actionable feedback and training signals, driving self-improvement loops, preference optimization, and prompt steering for the other agents that generate images and shape cinematography and color. And third, it's the Taste-Keeper. It personalizes. It stores whose aesthetic actually governs a given project, a director's lookbook, a brand's guidelines, an artist's portfolio, or an audience's preferences, and it conditions everything it does on that profile.

Now, why is this called a deep rethink rather than just a wrapper around existing tools? Because at every turn it pushes past the naive recipe. The simple guide might say, just train an aesthetic scorer that spits out a number. This agent treats a single number as both insufficient and dangerous. Instead it breaks aesthetics down into auditable sub-attributes, adds a temporal track for video, and attaches calibrated uncertainty to everything. The simple guide says, use the critic as a reward to fine-tune the generator. This agent wraps every reward in guardrails against reward hacking. The simple guide says, personalize on one artist's ratings. This agent makes taste a first-class, versioned, consent-governed profile. The simple guide works on still images. This agent is video-native from the start. And where the simple guide treats aesthetic sense as just predicting beauty, this agent treats it as beauty plus intent fidelity, plus hitting an emotional target, plus brand and style fit, plus novelty, all gated so that a high score can never come from generic, pretty, soulless output. The result is an agent that other agents genuinely cannot do their jobs without.

To understand the foundation, we need to talk about that phrase, artiste sense. The source survey it builds on defines it as the intuitive, perceptual, and expressive sensitivity that artists develop over time. It's an eye for composition, color harmony, rhythm, proportion, light and shadow, depth, and emotional resonance. It's a structural, almost three-dimensional way of seeing. It's a psychological drive to express something. And it's a grounded, iterative practice of making and remaking.

The central thesis, which this specification adopts wholesale, is this. An AI cannot possess genuine, lived artistic sense. It has no consciousness and no emotion. But it can develop a sophisticated simulated version through three things: data-driven evaluators trained on human aesthetic judgments, preference-alignment feedback loops, and computational-creativity extensions. And crucially, the strongest results come from human and AI working in symbiosis, not from autonomous AI artists working alone. The specification accepts that as ground truth and sets out to answer the one question the survey left open: what exactly is the buildable agent contract that delivers this inside a hundred-and-fourteen-agent video production system?

Out of that deep rethink came five architectural commitments, each a deliberate departure from the naive approach. The first: aesthetics is a vector, not a scalar. A single score from one to ten is unaccountable and trivially gamed. So the Critic emits a decomposed aesthetic vector, with independent heads for composition, color, light, depth, subject treatment, technical quality, emotional resonance, style fidelity, and novelty, each carrying its own confidence. Any single combined number is just a gated aggregation, never the source of truth.

The second commitment: aesthetics is temporal. This is a video swarm, and a montage of individually beautiful frames can still be aesthetically incoherent. So the Critic scores both per frame and per sequence, looking at motion smoothness, stability of color and exposure over time, the rhythm of the cuts against what the genre expects, and whether the clip reads as one authored gesture.

The third: aesthetics is always whose. Following a well-known critique that a one-size-fits-all beauty model just encodes a narrow, unexamined taste, the agent refuses to pretend there's one universal beauty. Every score is conditioned on an explicit aesthetic profile, whether that's a director, a brand, an artist, an audience cohort, or a labeled neutral baseline. Personalization is the default, not an add-on. Recent research even shows that personalized aesthetic models, built from interviewing someone about their taste, can beat generic models at predicting that individual's judgments.

The fourth: aesthetics must not be hackable. The moment the Critic becomes a reward, generators start trying to exploit it, with high-frequency texture spam, blown-out saturation, that slick AI-glossy sheen. So the Aligner ships with first-class anti-reward-hacking machinery: ensemble disagreement, reward-variance monitoring, anchoring to a reference model to prevent runaway exploitation, detectors for out-of-distribution artifacts, and inference-time mitigation. And it favors rich preferences that carry reasoning over opaque single numbers.

The fifth: aesthetics is gated by intent. Beauty divorced from the brief is just noise. So the overall quality is multiplied by intent fidelity, meaning does it actually match the shot intent, the prompt, the brand, and by emotional-target match, meaning does it land the intended feeling. A gorgeous image that ignores the director's intent scores low. That mirrors the value-gated selection used in the swarm's General Creative Agent.

Let's make the model concrete. Take an artifact, an image or a video clip, and evaluate it under three things: an aesthetic profile, an intent or brief, and an emotional target. The Critic produces that decomposed aesthetic vector across all the sub-attributes, each value between zero and one, each with its own confidence. Then the gated aesthetic quality is computed by taking the profile-weighted aggregate of the vector and multiplying it by intent fidelity, by emotion match, and by an anti-hack term that's one minus the likelihood the result is a hack or artifact. In plain terms, you start with a profiled blend of all the attribute scores, then you knock it down if it ignores the brief, knock it down if it misses the target emotion, and knock it down hard if it looks like the system was fooled rather than genuinely pleased. A high hack likelihood collapses the score no matter how pretty the surface looks.

The profile weights matter here. They come from whichever aesthetic profile is active. A brand might weight color and brand fit heavily. A horror director of photography might weight light and contrast above everything. Intent fidelity is measured by grounding the artifact against the shot-intent text or a reference, with a target threshold set by the Director Agent's rubric. Emotion match is a regression of valence and arousal against the target, shared with the Composer Agent's emotional-arc validator. And hack likelihood blends an out-of-distribution score, ensemble disagreement, and artifact detection.

For video, the quality is computed both per frame and at the sequence level, then combined, with one part weighting the average per-frame quality, another part weighting the temporal quality of the whole sequence, and a penalty subtracted for instability over time, things like flicker, color drift, or a character's identity breaking between frames. That last concern overlaps with the swarm's consistency agent.

There are a few hard principles baked right into the code. There's no naked scalar: any consumer asking for just the quality number also receives the full vector, the hack likelihood, and the top failing dimension. Uncertainty always travels: every score ships with confidence, and a low-confidence score must escalate to a human in the loop or a second model, never quietly pass. And it's profile-or-refuse: if no profile is supplied, the agent uses an explicitly labeled neutral baseline and flags that the result is taste-agnostic.

Architecturally, the Aesthetics Agent is one service made of three subsystems that share a single profile store and a single model registry. An artifact flows in, along with a profile, an intent, and an emotional target. The Critic perceives it with a multi-head evaluator. The Aligner takes that and refines, generating reward, preference, and critique signals. Surrounding both are the anti-hack guardrails, and beside them sits the Taste-Keeper holding the versioned profiles. Out the other end comes a structured aesthetic verdict, published onto a critique bus where consuming agents can react, plus any profile updates.

Inside the Critic is an ensemble of complementary parts. There's a fast regression head on a vision backbone for cheap first-pass screening at scale. There's a vision-language model critic, drawing on frontier vision models, that produces fine-grained, attribute-level critique in natural language along with scores, the explainable eye. And there are specialized detectors for artifacts, distorted hands and faces, color drift, exposure and zone analysis, compositional geometry like rule-of-thirds and leading lines, similarity against a style reference, and temporal smoothness using optical flow and video-quality metrics.

The Aligner converts verdicts into action in several forms. It produces self-refine feedback, a prioritized, machine-readable critique like, scene three is underexposed in this zone, the subject collides with the right edge, the color drifts across the cut. It produces a reward signal for various fine-tuning methods, always emitted with variance and ensemble-agreement metadata. It produces preference pairs for training, with rationale attached, not just a bare A beats B. And it produces prompt-steer hints, concrete prompt adjustments handed to the Prompt Engineer Agent to reach the target in three iterations or fewer.

The Taste-Keeper maintains the versioned, consent-governed profile store, built up through ingesting portfolios, collecting pairwise preferences, and running those LLM-based interview elicitations to surface someone's latent criteria.

Now let's look at the decomposed rubric in detail, because this is the heart of the Critic's eye. The Critic emits a score and a confidence for each dimension, and these are never collapsed before being logged. Composition measures balance, the rule of thirds, leading lines, negative space, framing, and clarity of staging, using a geometry detector plus the vision-language model. Color harmony measures palette coherence, contrast, temperature consistency, and mood, using color histograms, color-difference metrics, and palette extraction. Light and shadow looks at exposure zones, the ratio of key to fill, direction, dynamic range, and mood. Depth and form measures three-dimensional readability, layering, focal depth, and that structural way of seeing, using depth estimation. Subject treatment looks at how prominent the subject is, gaze, gesture, and silhouette readability, using saliency and pose detection. Technical quality covers sharpness, noise, banding, artifacts, and whether the resolution is adequate. Emotional resonance measures the valence and arousal it evokes against the target. Style fidelity measures adherence to the style bible, lookbook, or brand by comparing embeddings to a reference set. Novelty and distinctiveness measures originality against generic AI slop, and importantly it follows an inverted-U: it's rewarded only in a moderate band. And for video there's temporal aesthetics, covering motion smoothness, cut rhythm, stability over time, and that sense of one authored gesture.

That novelty point is worth dwelling on. Following the inverted-U principle from the Creative Agent spec, novelty is rewarded in a moderate range. Too low and you get cliché and derivative work. Too high and you get incoherence. That balance is exactly what stops the agent from rewarding bland, statistically-average, merely pretty output.

On the functional side, the input is a structured request that names the artifact, its media type, the profile to use, the intent including shot-intent text and any references and a genre prior, the emotional target as valence and arousal, the mode of operation, any constraints like aspect ratio, color space, or deliverable format, and a budget covering maximum latency and whether to use the fast or deep tier.

The output is the aesthetic verdict. It carries the artifact reference, the profile used, the full aesthetic vector across every dimension, the confidence for each, the intent-fidelity score, the emotion-match score, the hack likelihood, the final aesthetic quality, the top failing dimensions, a list of actionable critiques in plain language like, lift the key a third of a stop on the subject's left, or, the palette is conventional for this genre, consider one strategic outlier hue. It also carries prompt-steer hints, an uncertainty flag, a flag for whether to escalate to a human, and provenance recording which models were used and how much the ensemble agreed.

There are five modes. Screen is a fast scalar gate for culling high volumes of candidates using only the regression head. Score gives the full decomposed vector and verdict. Align emits the reward or preference signal for a training loop. Compare ranks several candidates against each other to choose the best take. And refine runs the full loop: generate, score, critique, suggest, repeated, mimicking the artist's own iterative process.

The agent is stateful. It keeps per-project memory of which artifacts were accepted or rejected, which ratchets the profile forward and feeds an episodic memory for ongoing learning, consistent with the swarm's principle of continuous self-improvement. On performance, fast-tier screening should come in under eight hundred milliseconds per artifact, deep-tier under eight seconds, it scales horizontally across GPUs, and it's deterministic given a fixed profile and fixed model versions, so audits are reproducible.

Integration is where this really becomes infrastructure. The agent is registered as cross-cutting, alongside the Research Agent, the General Creative Agent, and the Optimization Agent. Many agents call it. The Cinematographer Agent uses it to replace ad-hoc aesthetic scoring with a proper composition, light, and color self-refine rubric. The Colorist Agent uses it for color-drift, mood-vector, and palette-coherence scoring. The Storyboard, Concept Artist, and Production Design agents use it for style-bible adherence and composition. The Food Stylist, Travel Cinematography, and Real Estate Photo agents use it as the shared aesthetic regressor their specs reference. The Prompt Engineer Agent uses the refine mode and prompt-steer hints. The consistency agent uses it to cross-check temporal stability and artifacts. The Director and Judge agents use it to break ties and adjudicate blind preferences between candidate takes. And the delivery and marketing agents use it to score the aesthetics of thumbnails and hooks for predicted engagement.

Verdicts get published on the swarm's structured critique bus, tagged as aesthetic feedback with a severity, a rubric score, and the artifact reference, so any agent can react asynchronously. The verdict is also attached to the artifact's QC status field in the shared handoff contract, which means aesthetic state travels with provenance through every phase of production.

It's worth being clear about how this agent relates to its neighbors. Against the General Creative Agent: that one generates novel-yet-useful candidates, while the Aesthetics Agent judges and refines them, and in fact supplies the creative agent's novelty score. They form a generate-and-evaluate loop. Against the consistency agent: that one catches errors, like drift, broken hands, and identity breaks, while the Aesthetics Agent judges taste, with hack likelihood as the shared boundary they co-train on. And against the psychological recommendation and audience-simulation agents: those predict what an audience prefers, while the Aesthetics Agent encodes authorial or brand taste, though a profile can itself be an audience-cohort profile, which bridges the two.

There are three operating loops. The Critic loop simply evaluates: artifact in, ensemble score, decompose, gate by intent and emotion, anti-hack check, verdict out. The alignment loop teaches the generator, and it mirrors the source guide's pipeline but hardened. You generate several candidates from the base model. You rank them in compare mode and decompose them in score mode. Human raters spot-check a sampled subset, because this is symbiosis, not full autonomy. You build rich preference pairs with rationale and run a preference-optimization update. You monitor reward variance and ensemble agreement, and if the variance collapses you suspect hacking, freeze, and escalate. Then you repeat, and the generator gradually internalizes the taste and starts instinctively picking stronger outputs. The personalization loop captures whose taste it is. You ingest a portfolio, lookbook, or brand guide to seed an embedding profile. You collect pairwise preferences or run the interview elicitation to surface latent criteria. You fit the profile weights and validate them against held-out judgments. Then you version, sign, and store the profile, and expose its ID to the swarm.

That profile is itself a first-class, versioned, consent-governed object. It records the profile ID, the owner, the consent terms including scope, expiry, and a cryptographic signature, the dimension weights, exemplars and anti-exemplars, the elicited criteria in plain language like prefers low-key contrast or avoids saturated reds, an embedding centroid, a version number, and its full lineage. Profiles come in several types: Director, Brand, Artist, Audience-Cohort, Genre-prior, and Neutral-baseline. They're composable, so you can combine, say, a brand profile with a noir genre profile, with documented precedence rules. And governance is taken seriously: personalizing on a named human's taste or portfolio requires recorded consent in the profile, cleared by the Compliance Agent, with the same rigor applied to voice and likeness cloning.

Reward hacking deserves its own careful treatment, because it's the single greatest risk. The core problem: when the Critic becomes a reward, generators learn to fool the eye rather than please it. There are several failure modes. Texture and detail spam shows up as a high score paired with busy high-frequency noise; the defense is an artifact detector, a frequency-domain sanity check, and human spot-checks. Saturation and contrast blowout shows up as that oversaturated AI-glossy look; the defense is color-difference and gamut bounds, per-dimension caps, and brand constraints. Mode collapse shows up as all outputs converging to one safe look; the defense is reward-variance monitoring and large, diverse reward ensembles that retain variance. Off-distribution exploitation shows up as high scores on inputs unlike the training data; the defense is an out-of-distribution detector that forces escalation to a human and never auto-passes. Intent drift shows up as something beautiful that ignores the brief; the defense is that intent-fidelity gate multiplying the quality. Opaque-scalar overfit shows up as a generator gaming one number; the defense is rich, rationale-bearing preferences and a decomposed vector that's never collapsed. And taste monoculture or bias shows up as one narrow aesthetic encoded as universal; the defense is mandatory explicit profiles and bias audits.

Underneath those, four core mechanisms run continuously. Ensemble disagreement: the regression head, the vision-language critic, and the detectors all have to agree, and high disagreement raises the hack likelihood and triggers human review. Anchoring to a reference generator during alignment, to prevent runaway exploitation. Inference-time mitigation, so you can align without always doing full fine-tuning. And held-out human evaluation as the non-negotiable ground truth, where the agent's correlation with human raters is continuously monitored and serves as a release gate.

On the technical build, the core classes include the Aesthetic Critic, the attribute heads, the ensemble scorer, the intent gate, the emotion gate, the anti-hack guard, the Aligner, the preference builder, the Taste-Keeper, the profile object, and an Aesthetics Agent facade tying it together. The models include a vision backbone with an MLP regression head for the fast tier, a vision-language critic via the swarm's LLM providers, and a zoo of detectors. The frameworks include PyTorch with diffusion tooling for the alignment hooks, a graph node for orchestration, a web service layer, and a streaming layer for publishing to the critique bus. The stores include a vector database for exemplars and embeddings, a versioned and signed profile database, and episodic memory for the refine loop. Provenance is logged on every verdict, recording model versions, ensemble agreement, and profile version into an audit trail. And the deliverables include a repo skeleton, the verdict and profile schemas, example notebooks for image scoring, video scoring, the alignment loop, and profile elicitation, plus integration adapters for the key consuming agents.

How do we know it's working? The success criteria are concrete. Correlation with held-out human aesthetic ratings should reach a Spearman value of at least point seven five on the benchmark, and at least point eight five on a personalized profile. The personalized model should beat the neutral baseline at predicting the owner's pairwise choices. Outputs aligned through this agent should win at least fifty-five percent of blind pairwise comparisons against unaligned outputs. Reward variance should be maintained and mode-collapse kept below threshold across a fine-tuning run. The hack-likelihood signal should catch at least ninety-five percent of human-flagged cases where the eye was fooled. Latency should stay within the fast and deep tier targets. Confidence should be well-calibrated, with low-confidence cases reliably escalating. And every single verdict should carry the decomposed vector and full provenance.

The specification is honest about its limits. There's no genuine lived aesthetic here; the agent's sense is statistical and derivative, with no spontaneity or personal impulse. It's an amplifier of human taste, best used in symbiosis, with a human in the loop on novel or low-confidence judgments. Taste is contested, and even with explicit profiles the corpus and the rater pool carry bias, so the agent surfaces whose taste it encodes rather than claiming universality. Reward hacking is an arms race, where the defenses reduce but never fully eliminate exploitation, which is why held-out human evaluation remains the ground truth and the release gate. Looking ahead, the future directions include larger multimodal critics, biosignal proxies for emotional resonance, more embodied and three-dimensional structural seeing, tighter artist co-training loops, and cross-modal aesthetics that span image, music, and motion together, shared with the Composer and Choreography agents.

And that's the whole picture. This specification takes the survey of artiste sense and transforms it into something buildable and integrated into the swarm: a decomposed multimodal Critic, a hack-resistant Aligner, and a consent-governed Taste-Keeper. Together they form the shared aesthetic nervous system of the entire system. Implemented as written, the Aesthetics Agent gives all hundred and fourteen agents a single, trustworthy, personalizable eye.
