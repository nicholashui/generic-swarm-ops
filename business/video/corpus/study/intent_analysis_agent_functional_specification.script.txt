Every time someone writes or speaks, there's more going on than the words on the surface. Language always has a purpose. It carries hidden motives, it can be read from many angles, and it almost always has a moral dimension to it. So here's a question worth sitting with. Why does a particular piece of language even exist? What is its real goal? What is being hidden? How many different perspectives are baked into it? And is the behavior behind it good, harmful, or simply effective? That's the heart of what this project sets out to answer, and the answer takes the shape of a complete system called the Deep Intent Analysis Framework, version two.

Think of it as a production-ready, modular machine for decoding any text. Its job is to systematically uncover a text's purpose, its hidden agenda, its many angles, the force of what it's trying to do to you, and the ethical quality of how it does it. The original version was a six-phase pipeline that a human or a language model could follow by hand, almost like a recipe. This new version turns that recipe into a fully specified, agent-driven software system, built on top of one of the most capable models available, with native tool use, an enormous context window of more than a million tokens, structured outputs, and reasoning that stays grounded and resists making things up.

The goals are ambitious but clear. The framework wants to reach human-expert-level reasoning about meaning and intent, but at scale, and it wants to work everywhere, whether you're using it by hand, through an interface, in a web application, as a coding plugin, or inside enterprise analytics.

So what's new in this second version, and where does it come from? The upgrades are drawn from a wave of recent research in computational pragmatics, speech act theory, and how language models reason about implied meaning. The first big addition is something called the Pragmatic Inference Chain, a method that dramatically sharpens the system's ability to catch implied meaning and hidden agendas. The second is multi-perspective agent simulation, inspired by research into conversations involving many participants, which lets the system map out far richer angles. The third draws on the classic conversational maxims described by the philosopher Grice, the unspoken rules of cooperative communication, and then extends them with two new maxims tailored for an age of artificial intelligence: benevolence and transparency. On top of that, there's automated classification of speech acts, a hybrid evaluation pipeline that blends automatic metrics with human judgment, and deep integration with the underlying model's reasoning modes and real-time search.

Who is this for? Journalists, analysts, researchers, educators, content moderators, legal teams, AI safety engineers, and anyone who simply wants to see through language rather than be carried along by it.

Let's talk about the foundation, because this isn't built on intuition alone. Several key research sources shape the design. One is a broad survey of pragmatics in the era of large language models, which gathered datasets for speech acts and implied meaning, and pointed out where these models still fall short, especially their bias toward English and their struggle with deeper reasoning. It recommended hybrid evaluation and prompting grounded in real theory. Another is the Pragmatic Inference Chain itself, a four-step method rooted in relevance theory that produced a striking improvement in detecting hidden toxicity and bias. It works by carefully separating the literal meaning from the metaphorical, identifying where social or ethical norms are violated, and only then arriving at a judgment. A third source is a survey of multi-party conversational agents, which contributed a taxonomy of mental states, ideas about discourse structure, and the concept of theory of mind, the ability to model what others are thinking, which makes true multi-angle simulation possible. And finally, the research on the underlying model itself shows real strength in agent-style tool use, long-context reasoning, low hallucination, and the ability to play different social roles, from truth arbiter to adversary, which makes it a trustworthy backbone for this kind of work.

Now let's walk through how the system is actually structured, because it flows like an assembly line. A user provides some text, optionally with context. That text first reaches the context analyzer. Then it moves to a purpose classifier. From there, a surface-level parser reads the literal meaning. Next comes the pragmatic engine, the real workhorse, which handles speech acts, the inference chain, and the detection of violated conversational maxims. After that, a hidden agenda and multi-angle dissector pulls the text apart from many viewpoints at once. Then a judgment engine scores the behavior and audits its ethics. And finally, a synthesizer pulls everything together and recommends what to do. The end result is a structured data output, a human-readable report, and visualizations.

Underneath, the core components are straightforward. The brain is the language model, whose reasoning depth can be dialed from low to medium to high. Around it sits a tool layer with real-time search for checking context, a code interpreter for metrics and clustering, and enforcement of structured output. There's an optional layer of classical language processing for baseline classifiers, similarity between angles, and topic modeling. An orchestration framework coordinates everything with parallel tool calls. Storage keeps analysis history, user templates, and benchmark results in a database, including a vector database for similarity search. And the system reaches users through many doors: an interface for developers, a web app, a command line, a coding editor extension, and chat bots. Crucially, every phase hands typed artifacts to the next one, so you can trace every conclusion back to the evidence and the confidence behind it.

Let's go through the six phases in more detail, because each one does real work. Phase zero is the context analyzer. It takes the raw text plus any metadata, like who sent it, on what platform, and when, and it extracts the sender's power and relationship to the audience, the norms of the medium, and what triggered the message. It even runs a bias self-check, and it can reach out for historical or cultural context in real time. Its output is a structured context object with a confidence level.

Phase one is purpose categorization. Drawing on a classic model of language functions, it sorts the text across categories like referential, emotive, persuasive, social-contact, language-about-language, and poetic. In this version it does multi-label classification, assigning probabilities and justifying each one with evidence.

Phase two is surface literal analysis. This is the careful read of what's actually said: separating facts from opinions, spotting loaded words, noticing the voice, catching what's been left out, and flagging ambiguity. It's enhanced with vagueness scoring and the resolution of who or what each reference points to.

Phase three is the pragmatic and speech act engine, and this is the core upgrade. It uses the classic five categories of speech acts, things like asserting, directing, committing, expressing, and declaring, plus finer-grained labels like attack and defend drawn from debate and online aggression datasets. It runs an automated detector for violations of the conversational maxims of quantity, quality, relevance, and manner, plus the extended maxims of benevolence and transparency. And it applies the Pragmatic Inference Chain, which is mandatory for hidden agendas. That chain has four steps: first, explain any metaphors or special meanings in plain language; second, state the literal meaning in plain language; third, identify contradictions with relevant social or ethical norms, like equality, truth-seeking, and freedom from manipulation; and fourth, deliver a final judgment about the implied meaning. For every utterance it produces the act being performed, its likely effect on the listener, the maxims it violates, the implied meaning, and a confidence score.

Phase four is the hidden agenda and multi-angle dissection, and this is a major upgrade. It begins by separating explicit goals from implicit ones, like seeking power, status, division, or validation. Then it scans for power and ideology in the style of critical discourse analysis, asking who benefits and whether there's an us-versus-them framing. The new heart of this phase is multi-perspective agent simulation. The system spawns four to six parallel agents, each one embodying a single point of view: the speaker, the receiver, an opponent, society at large, a historical lens, or an environmental one. Each agent produces its own summary of the angle along with evidence, and the results are clustered together. The phase finishes with framing analysis, inconsistency detection, and a classification of the agenda type, whether it's there to inform, persuade, manipulate, bond, signal, or deceive, each with its own sub-score. The output names the hidden agenda, counts the angles, describes each one with its framing and evidence, and importantly, lists the angles that were ignored.

Phase five is the behavioral judgment engine. It scores the text across eight dimensions. Six are core: truthfulness, ethical impact including harm and autonomy and power imbalance, effectiveness against both stated and hidden goals, clarity and cooperation, social value measured as understanding versus division, and transparency. Two are new: benevolence, which is specific to AI contexts, and cultural appropriateness, drawn from multi-task pragmatic models. The engine delivers a final verdict that is part category, part narrative, and part recommended action. And there's a strict rule: every single score must cite evidence from the earlier phases.

Phase six is synthesis. It produces a one-paragraph executive summary, actionable recommendations, and a confidence vector. Optionally, it can generate counter-arguments or a guide for how to respond.

The output itself is captured in a clean, structured format. For each analysis it records a unique identifier, the input text, the context, the purpose with its dominant functions and their scores, the surface reading, the pragmatic findings including speech acts and maxim violations and the inferred implicature, the hidden agenda with its angle count and perspectives, and the judgment with all its scores, a verdict, and a recommended action. A sample verdict might read: strategically effective, but ethically manipulative. Each record also carries an overall confidence, the processing time, and the model and reasoning mode that produced it.

The non-functional requirements keep the system honest. On performance, it aims to analyze a text of under five thousand tokens in less than fifteen seconds at high reasoning, running phases in parallel. On scalability, the interface is stateless and there's a batch mode for processing thousands of texts in an enterprise setting. On accuracy, the target is more than eighty-five percent agreement with expert linguists on speech act and implicature benchmarks. On explainability, every claim comes with an evidence span and a confidence level. On ethics and safety, there's built-in refusal of harmful misuse, ongoing bias auditing, and alignment toward truth-seeking and genuine helpfulness without flattery. And on privacy, there's an optional local mode and no lasting storage of user text unless someone opts in.

There's a clear roadmap to build it. The first stretch, roughly the first four weeks, is a prompt-engineered prototype covering all six phases with the inference chain and basic multi-agent behavior, with validated structured output. The second stretch, across a couple of months, fine-tunes a lightweight speech act classifier on public datasets and folds in classical metrics. The third stretch, over the following months, delivers full agent orchestration, a web interface, a developer interface, and a benchmark suite covering accuracy, latency, and user studies. And the fourth stretch is ongoing: continuous evaluation against new datasets and contributions back to the community. The technology stack pairs a Python backend and the model's software kit with an agent framework, a modern web frontend, a relational database alongside a vector store, and a custom evaluation harness built on real pragmatic datasets.

Evaluation happens on several fronts. Automatically, it measures performance on speech act datasets, the lift from the inference chain, and how well maxim-violation detection correlates with human labels. With humans, it targets strong agreement with expert linguists. Through A/B testing, it pits the framework against a baseline model on real-world material like news, advertising, political speech, and social media posts. And specific to the underlying model, it measures the reduction in flattery and hallucination during the judgment phase.

The framework is honest about its limits. It's still English-dominant, though multilingual inference prompts help. It needs good context to do its best work. And it costs more compute on very long documents, though the huge context window and summarization agents soften that. Looking ahead, the roadmap imagines extending into video and audio, monitoring live multi-party conversations in real time, fine-tuning a specialist model, and contributing an open benchmark suite back to the research community.

In the end, this document is meant to be actionable. You could drop it straight into a product requirements document, hand it to engineers to build, or use it as the very system prompt that powers the analysis agent itself. The natural next step is simple: provide a sample text, and the full pipeline can run on it, end to end, decoding not just what the language says, but why it exists, what it hides, and whether it deserves your trust.
