Imagine an entire film and video production studio, but instead of people filling every chair, each role is handled by a specialized artificial intelligence agent. That's the idea behind this document. It's a roster, a kind of organizational chart, that lays out a complete swarm of AI agents and shows how they fit together to make professional-grade video. The roster is split into categories, so let's walk through them the way you'd tour a working studio, department by department.

This particular version of the roster was distilled from a larger video production workflow document, and it deliberately restores an older, cleaner layout as its backbone, then folds the newer workflow-support material back in. The goal is one consistent structure that's easy to follow.

For every single agent, the document tracks the same set of attributes. It names what the agent is responsible for. It lists where the agent's expertise comes from, the licensed and consented sources it learned from. It spells out how the agent checks its own quality. It defines the signal that proves the agent can actually beat a skilled human at the task. It says which other agents are allowed to critique its work, and whose work it's allowed to critique in return. It lists the tools the agent can reach for. And finally it names the underlying reasoning pattern that powers it. Keep those nine attributes in mind, because they repeat all the way down the list.

Let's start at the top, with what the document calls the above-the-line agents. These are the leadership roles. The Director agent owns the creative vision; it issues the intent for each shot, sets the pacing, and approves takes. It learned from director interviews, film commentary, and master classes from the likes of Scorsese, Lynch, and Gerwig, and it measures success by how faithfully each shot matches its intent and whether it can win blind head-to-head comparisons against cuts made by credentialed human directors. Alongside it sits the Producer, or executive producer, who handles budget, schedule, hiring, and delivery, and who greenlights each phase of the project. There's the Screenwriter, who turns a treatment into a full screenplay with structure and distinctive dialogue, learning from celebrated scripts and from craft books like Robert McKee's Story. There's the Showrunner, who keeps story arcs consistent across many episodes and orchestrates the writers' room. And there's the Casting agent, which selects voices and likenesses and simulates auditions, while making sure consent is fully respected.

Next come the camera and lighting agents. The Cinematographer, or director of photography, handles lensing, lighting, composition, and the overall look, drawing on decades of cinematography journals and theory. The Camera Operator executes the actual framing, focus, and movement that the cinematographer asks for. And the Drone Pilot handles aerial work, whether simulated or real, with safety rules like aviation regulations baked in as guiding principles so it never crosses a line.

Then we reach editorial and color, which is a large group. The Editor assembles the cut, manages pacing, and selects coverage, guided by the legendary editing wisdom of Walter Murch. The Colorist handles the final grade and keeps the look consistent, watching tiny color differences and protecting accurate skin tones. There's a Visual Effects Supervisor who plans and oversees the effects pipeline, an Animator focused on character motion, weight, and timing, and a Motion Graphics agent for kinetic typography, lower thirds, and infographics. There's a Storyboard agent that turns the script into shot panels, a Concept Artist for pre-production world and character design, a Production Designer for sets and locations, a Costume Designer who tells character through wardrobe, and a makeup, hair, and special-effects agent that guards facial and hair continuity across takes.

The sound and music department comes next. The Sound Design agent builds ambience, foley, and effects, syncing them precisely and hitting broadcast loudness targets. The Composer writes the original score, aligning every musical cue to the intended emotion. The Voice-Over agent handles narration, character voices, and ad reads. And the Sound Mixer delivers the final mix in formats like 5.1 and Atmos, meeting broadcast standards on the first pass.

After that come performance and choreography agents. There's a Choreography agent for movement design in music videos and dance challenges, a Music Video Director for the visual concept behind songs, a Comedy Writer for skits, parody, and viral material, an on-camera Talent agent that delivers AI-rendered performance, and a user-generated-content Creator agent that produces authentic-feeling ads in a creator's voice at a tiny fraction of the usual cost.

The distribution and marketing group handles getting the work seen. A Social Media Strategist tailors distribution to each platform and times posts around trends. A Copywriter crafts scripts, captions, hooks, and headlines that match the brand voice. A Creative Director shapes the overarching campaign concept. And a Performance Marketer optimizes ads for return on ad spend, learning from platform feedback.

Then there's a broad education and domain-expert group, and this is where the system reaches into specialized knowledge. An Instructional Design agent turns learning objectives into scripts and assessments. A Subject-Matter Expert agent guarantees accuracy in a target field, aiming to pass the same certifications a human professional would, whether that's finance, medicine, or cloud computing. A Fact-Checker grades the source of every claim. There are specialists for medical illustration, journalism with ethical framing, legal compliance covering regulations like the EU AI Act and privacy law, finance, food styling, travel cinematography, children's authorship with strict safety, audiobook narration, sign-language interpretation reviewed by the Deaf community, localization quality, and even real-estate photography and 3D scanning. Each one is benchmarked against credentialed human experts.

The document then describes a set of AI-era specialists, roles that simply didn't exist before generative tools. There's a Prompt Engineer who steers the big video generators to hit a target shot in just a few tries. There's an Avatar Designer for synthetic presenters, built around consent and cryptographic provenance. There's a Voice Clone and lip-sync specialist, again gated on verified consent. There's a quality-and-consistency agent that catches frame drift, distorted hands and faces, and identity breaks. There's a Personalization engineer for templates that swap in a viewer's name, face, or voice. There's a Trailer Editor focused on hook-driven cuts. And there's a Sports Analyst that builds tactical breakdowns and on-screen diagrams.

Beyond the craft roles, there's a whole layer of what the document calls specialist meta-agents, the agents that coordinate other agents. The orchestration group includes an Orchestrator that runs the workflow graph with retries, timeouts, and fan-out and fan-in; a Planner that breaks a brief into a phased plan with critique gates; a Router that picks the right specialist and model for each subtask; a Judge that settles disputes through structured debate and scores work against a rubric; a Gatekeeper that controls phase transitions and signs off on provenance; and a Memory agent that holds the project's episodic and long-term memory so any other agent can retrieve what it needs.

There's a creative meta-group too, with an Ideation agent for divergent brainstorming, a Narrative Arc agent for story structure, a Style Transfer agent that applies a named aesthetic consistently, a Mood Board agent, a Novelty or anti-cliché critic that flags tired tropes, and an Emotional Arc agent that maps the rise and fall of feeling across a piece.

A research meta-group covers live web research, deep archival and academic search, trend intelligence that spots emerging memes and sounds early, competitor intelligence, citation grading, interview synthesis, and benchmark research that watches the leaderboards.

And an optimization meta-group continuously tunes the system: a Prompt Optimizer that auto-improves prompts, a Cost Optimizer that routes between models for the best price-to-quality, a Latency Optimizer for speed, a Retention Optimizer that tunes hooks and pacing for watch time, a return-on-ad-spend optimizer, an Accessibility Optimizer that enforces contrast, captions, and audio description, an Evaluation Harness that runs benchmarks and flags regressions, and a Safety Red Team that adversarially probes for deepfakes, bias, jailbreaks, and defamation.

Finally there's a large workflow-support group, the connective tissue that turns finished creative into a shipped, compliant product. It includes an Analyst that turns telemetry into decision-ready reports, an Audience Simulator that predicts how viewers will react, a dedicated Accessibility agent that owns final acceptance, a Brand agent and a Brand Strategist, a Marketing agent, a search-optimization agent, a Community agent that listens to audience response, template and user-experience reviewers, a Trust and Safety agent, a customer-relationship agent, a Legal agent for novel high-risk issues, festival and awards strategists, a Critic that simulates press and jury reactions, learning-management and learner-simulation agents, a Continuity agent, a dedicated Lip-Sync gate, music supervision and label-side roles, a Deepfake Detection agent, a Communications agent, archive producers, a Standards Editor, an Ethics agent, a Channel Manager, a Corrections agent, a ratings-packaging agent, sales and distribution agents, and an Archive Master that produces preservation-grade masters. All told, the roster runs to a hundred and fourteen distinct agents.

After the roster, the document steps back to describe the common structure that every one of these agents shares, no matter which department it sits in. Think of it as a single skeleton repeated everywhere. The accompanying architecture diagram presents the agent not as a loose sketch but as a governed operating system, showing how orchestration, the input contract, knowledge and tool surfaces, the internal plan-act-and-self-review loop, traceability controls, a three-layer quality gate, release packaging, peer critique, human escalation, and continuous improvement all work together as one system.

Breaking that skeleton into its parts, every agent has a stable identity, expressed as a versioned handle and registered so the router can find it. It has a single, clearly scoped responsibility that mirrors a human craft role, which prevents overlap. It has its knowledge distillation source, the licensed corpora it's grounded on, refreshed over time. It has tool access through a shared open protocol, reaching video generators, voice tools, and professional editing software. It runs on one or more named reasoning patterns. It has memory, held in a vector database with a hierarchical, summarize-and-evict design. And it carries a written constitution or rubric, a role-specific scoring guide, things like Murch's Rule of Six for the editor, the twelve principles of animation for the animator, or accessibility standards for the accessibility agent.

The quality checking happens in three layers. The first layer is specification: did the output meet the structured brief, validated automatically against schemas and technical checks, which it must pass completely. The second layer is rubric: does it meet the craft standard for the role, judged by an AI acting as judge against that written constitution, and it has to score at least eighty-five out of a hundred, with up to three self-refinement passes if it falls short. The third layer is preference: would the target audience actually choose this over a human-made baseline, tested through a simulated audience panel plus a smaller set of human samples, where a fifty-percent win rate means parity and fifty-five percent means it surpasses the human.

Each agent also carries a pre-registered surpass-human signal, concrete proof that it exceeds a credentialed professional, whether through benchmark dominance, blind preference wins, the same quality delivered far faster, a lower long-term defect rate, or passing a real certification. It has a critique inbox and a critique outbox, both running on a shared structured message bus, where feedback is rated by severity. A blocker halts the whole workflow, a major issue triggers self-refinement, and minor notes are logged to improve the model later. Disputes escalate to the Judge agent, and if they still can't be resolved, to a human.

That human-in-the-loop escalation is reserved for the moments that genuinely need a person: consent matters, final legal sign-off, content ratings, festival eligibility, crisis communications, and cross-cultural sensitivity. Every artifact the system emits is cryptographically signed for provenance, so downstream agents can verify the chain and platforms can automatically label AI-generated content. And every agent keeps improving after deployment, moving from an initial grounding in licensed material, through paid and consented expert interviews, into live reinforcement learning, award-rubric grounding, adversarial red-teaming, and reality checks at thirty, sixty, and ninety days against real-world results like retention and return on ad spend. Each agent plugs into the larger graph as a node, where the orchestrator schedules it, the planner assigns it, the router selects its model, and the gatekeeper verifies all three quality layers before the work is allowed to advance.

The document also defines the shape of a critique message in plain terms: it carries who it's from and who it's to, which artifact it refers to, how severe it is, what category it falls under, the evidence behind it, the suggested action, a reference to the relevant rubric, and the deadline by which it must be resolved. And it sketches how everything composes together: a brief flows into the planner, then the orchestrator, then the router, which fans the work out to the dozens of craft agents, while the memory agent, the gatekeeper, the judge, and the critique messages all loop around to keep the system honest, supported by the creative, research, and optimization meta-agents.

The document closes with its references, and they're worth noting because they ground the whole design in real research. The reasoning patterns trace back to published work: Self-Refine, where an agent drafts, critiques itself against a rubric, and revises without retraining; Reflexion, which stores verbal self-reflection in memory to do better next time; Constitutional AI, where the reward comes from an AI critic governed by a written constitution; multi-agent debate, which improves factual accuracy and reasoning; the idea of using a large language model as a judge, which agrees with human preferences more than eighty percent of the time; ReAct, which interleaves reasoning with tool use; and agentic graph frameworks for orchestration, along with automated prompt optimization methods. The references also list the evaluation benchmarks for video and language quality, the generative video models that make up the tooling landscape, the voice and avatar tools, and the infrastructure standards, including the content-provenance standard that regulators are beginning to mandate and the open protocol that connects models to tools. It's less a list of tools and more a blueprint for how an entire creative organization could run.
