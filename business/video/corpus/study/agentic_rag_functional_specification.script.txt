Imagine you've got a mountain of knowledge sitting on your hard drive. Tens of thousands of documents, half a gigabyte of text, and somewhere inside it are the answers to almost any question you could ask. The problem is, how do you actually get to those answers in a way that's smart, reliable, and doesn't just make things up? That's the challenge this project sets out to solve, and the solution is something called an Agentic Retrieval-Augmented Generation system. In this video, we're going to walk through the complete blueprint for building one, and it's an ambitious one.

So let's start with the big picture. The goal here is to deliver a complete, production-ready system that can be observed, measured, extended, and benchmarked. It's not a toy. It's designed to faithfully implement four core agentic design patterns and seven architectural elements that come straight out of a research survey on Agentic RAG. The whole thing is expected to take somewhere between ten and fourteen days to build, with a working core ready in about six days, and then the remaining time spent on scaling it up, integrating the more advanced pieces, and adding the observability and benchmarking that make it trustworthy.

Now, what does this system actually have to do? First, it needs to natively read and index around sixty-five thousand Markdown files, a corpus of roughly five hundred megabytes. And it has to do that using a technique called hierarchical chunking, in a way that's careful with memory, processes things incrementally, and can resume if it gets interrupted. Second, it has to support hybrid knowledge representation. That means combining a vector store, called Chroma, with a second system called LightRAG, which builds an entity and relationship graph and allows for a smarter, dual-level kind of retrieval. Third, there's an optional feature for persistent knowledge compounding, inspired by an idea from Andrej Karpathy, where the system can write out its own growing wiki of concepts, complete with an index, a log, and linked notes. And finally, the whole thing should be local-first, packaged in Docker, fully traceable, and hardened for production use.

This specification is the result of more than ten rounds of refinement. It started as a simple request for an Agentic RAG system, then grew to include a comparison with the wiki idea, then the LightRAG enhancement, then the scaling up for sixty-five thousand files, and repeated requests for ever deeper design detail. So what you're hearing is the distilled, definitive version.

Let's talk about the core concepts, because these are non-negotiable. There are four core agentic design patterns, and each one must show up clearly in the system's behavior. The first is reflection. This is where the agents evaluate their own output, checking for relevance, faithfulness, and whether they've hallucinated anything, and then they iterate to improve. The second is planning, where the system autonomously breaks a complex question down into smaller sub-tasks or a multi-step plan. The third is tool use, where the agent dynamically calls tools in an interleaved way, following a think, act, observe rhythm. And the fourth is multi-agent collaboration, where specialized agents work together, sharing state, sometimes with a supervisor overseeing them, sometimes as equal peers.

On top of those four patterns sit seven architectural elements. The first is routing, where a single agent decides where to send a query, and can delegate to others. The second is hierarchical, graph-based control flow that governs how the whole process executes. The third is adaptive retrieval, where the complexity of the query determines which strategy gets used. The fourth is stateful memory, which holds onto the conversation, the long-term index, and checkpoints along the way. The fifth is hybrid knowledge, combining the vector store, a lightweight knowledge graph, and the persistent Markdown wiki. The sixth is iterative refinement, with quality gates and a sensible limit on how many times it loops. And the seventh is being evaluation-aware, with built-in metrics, tracing, and health checks baked right in.

What makes this approach stand out? Well, it's better than naive RAG because it adds genuine agency, the ability to reason and act. It's better than a pure wiki because it brings agentic reasoning at the moment you ask a question, with the option to write findings back. And LightRAG gives it fast relational power without the heavy cost of rebuilding a full knowledge graph from scratch.

Now let's look at how the system is actually wired together. A user asks a question, either from the command line or from a web interface. That question first hits a router, a query analyzer that picks the right strategy. From there it goes to a planner agent, which decomposes the query and builds a multi-hop plan. The plan flows into a tool router, which makes a structured decision about which tools to use, whether that's the vector retriever, LightRAG, a web search, or the wiki. The vector retriever pulls from Chroma using a technique that balances relevance and diversity, along with hierarchical chunks and reranking. The LightRAG retriever works across both entities and relationships. And the web search tool reaches out to the live internet when needed.

Everything those retrievers find then goes to a researcher and grader agent. This is where reflection happens. The agent scores each document against a rubric, and if the collection of results isn't good enough, specifically if the grade falls below eighty-five percent and it hasn't yet looped three times, it sends the work back to the planner to try again. Once the results are strong enough, they pass to the generator agent, which synthesizes an answer complete with citations. Then a critic agent independently checks that answer for faithfulness and hallucination. If it fails, the work goes back to the researcher. If it passes, you get the final answer with its citations. And optionally, a wiki synthesizer agent can take that result and produce a persistent, Karpathy-style record in the wiki vault, updating the index, the log, and the concept notes. Running underneath all of this is the state and memory layer, which keeps checkpoints, conversation summaries, and the long-term index, plus the hybrid knowledge layer holding both the Chroma vector database and the LightRAG knowledge graph.

The system relies on carefully defined data models. There's a structure for each retrieved document that records the document itself, a relevance score between zero and one, where it came from, whether it's a parent or child chunk, the headers it falls under, and any entities or relations LightRAG extracted from it. And there's a broader agent state that tracks the messages, the original query, the plan, the retrieved documents, any critique, a confidence score, the number of iterations, the final answer, the citations, the path to any wiki output, the LightRAG context, and assorted metadata like timestamps and tracing information. The number of iterations is deliberately capped at three, so the system can't loop forever.

Each node in the graph has a clear job. The query analyzer classifies how complex a question is, simple and factual versus multi-hop and relational, and decides on routing. The planner outputs a numbered plan or a set of sub-queries, using examples to handle multi-hop questions, like turning "compare X and Y" into separate sub-queries plus a synthesis step. The tool router produces structured output that selects tools and their parameters, and it can run tools in parallel where that's safe. The researcher and grader executes the chosen retrievers, grades each document on relevance, completeness, recency, and authority, filters out anything below a threshold, and reflects if the results are weak. The generator uses the filtered documents, the plan, and any critique to produce a grounded answer with inline citations. The critic independently scores faithfulness and can trigger another loop if the score is low. And the wiki synthesizer generates clean, Obsidian-compatible notes with proper frontmatter and wiki links, updating the index and log as it goes. Every one of these nodes has its own prompt, with a strict role, a detailed rubric or output format, a handful of examples both positive and negative, and encouragement to think step by step where reflection and planning are involved.

Scaling this to sixty-five thousand files is one of the hardest parts, so the ingestion pipeline gets special attention. A loader reads all the Markdown files using multiple worker processes, handling them in batches of a few thousand at a time. Then comes the hierarchical chunking, considered best practice for Markdown. First, the text is split by its headers, creating larger parent chunks of a few thousand tokens each, with the full header path stored alongside them. Then those parent chunks are split again into smaller child chunks of a few hundred tokens, with a bit of overlap, and the two are linked together by a shared identifier. The vector indexing loads everything into Chroma, and the LightRAG indexing follows, extracting entities and relationships asynchronously and using an OpenSearch backend to handle the scale. The whole process is resumable, tracking which files it has already handled by their hashes and cleaning up memory after each batch. The target is impressive: a full ingestion in under forty-five minutes on a machine with thirty-two gigabytes of RAM, and incremental updates for small changes in under a minute.

When it comes to retrieval, the vector search pulls a set of candidates and reranks them. LightRAG runs in a hybrid mode that combines low-level entities with high-level relationships. And the adaptive router leans toward LightRAG whenever a query involves comparison, asks how something works, or asks about relationships and connections between things.

The system comes with a set of tools that are both dynamic and extensible: a hybrid retrieve function, a web search through Tavily, a wiki writer, and as bonuses, a calculator and an arXiv fetcher. The graph itself is built around the agent state, with conditional edges that drive the reflection loop based on confidence and iteration count, parallel tool execution where possible, and full tracing on every single node.

On the surface, users interact through a couple of channels. There's a Streamlit web app with a chat interface, an expandable view of the system's reasoning step by step showing scores, documents, and critiques, and a button to save results to the wiki. There's also a command-line interface that lets you ingest with resume support, run queries with optional hybrid mode, wiki writing, and tracing, lint the corpus, run evaluations, and build the wiki. Speaking of evaluation, there's a dedicated harness that measures faithfulness, answer relevancy, and context precision, along with a custom reflection score, tested against a golden set of more than fifty queries with automated runs and JSON reports. Observability comes through a tracing project for every run with custom metadata for the agentic metrics. Everything is packaged in a multi-container Docker setup, and error handling includes graceful fallbacks, retry logic, and rate limiting.

The build follows a strict, phased order. Phase zero lays down the project skeleton, the requirements, the configuration, the data models, the prompt templates, and the Docker setup. Phase one builds the ingestion pipeline and benchmarks it against the full sixty-five thousand files, including the incremental mode and LightRAG indexing. Phase two implements the hybrid retriever and the tools. Phase three builds the core graph, with its state, nodes, edges, and the reflection and planning loops, plus memory. Phase four adds the multi-agent collaboration, the critic, and the wiki synthesizer. Phase five brings in the user interface, the command-line tool, the tracing visualization, and the evaluation harness. Phase six handles Docker, tests, logging, security, and documentation, including diagrams, the comparison table, and benchmarks. And phase seven is end-to-end stress testing with a hundred complex queries, latency and quality benchmarks, and a final polish.

So how do we know if we've succeeded? The criteria are concrete and measurable. The full corpus must ingest incrementally without running out of memory or crashing, with the benchmark logged. Every complex query should visibly demonstrate all four patterns and all seven elements. The reflection loop should trigger on at least thirty percent of queries and measurably improve the quality. LightRAG's hybrid mode should clearly outperform pure vector search on relational and multi-hop questions. The wiki synthesis should produce a clean, ready-to-use vault. On evaluation, faithfulness should reach at least ninety-two percent, answer relevancy at least ninety percent, and average latency should stay under four seconds on ordinary consumer hardware. And the code should be clean, fully typed, documented, and committed to version control at every phase.

The blueprint points to its sources: the original survey paper, the accompanying video, the LightRAG project with its OpenSearch support, Karpathy's wiki gist for the output style, and the latest LangGraph examples. The immediate action is to start with phases zero and one right away, focusing on robust, resumable ingestion of that massive corpus first. Once ingestion is complete and benchmarked, the next step is a detailed review of the prompts and a session to wire up the graph. This is the canonical, ultra-detailed, production-grade plan for an Agentic RAG system, combining hybrid retrieval with persistent, compounding knowledge. The instruction at the end is simple: ship it clean, observable, and performant.
