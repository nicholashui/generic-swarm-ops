Humans have spent thousands of years figuring out how to think well. From ancient philosophy to modern complexity science, we've built dozens of frameworks for making decisions, solving problems, and learning from our mistakes. So here's a fascinating question. If you were designing an artificial agent, a system that reasons, acts, observes, and refines its own behavior in a loop, which of those human thinking models would actually be worth borrowing? In this video, we're going to walk through a ranked list of forty traditional thinking models, scored from one to ten based on how much value each one could add to an agent's reasoning loop. Let's count down from the very top.

Sitting right at the summit, with a perfect score of ten, is the Cynefin Framework. This comes from complexity science, developed by Dave Snowden. Its big idea is that not all problems are the same. Some are simple, some are complicated, some are genuinely complex, and some are pure chaos. The skill is sensing which kind of situation you're in and then responding in a way that fits. For an agent, this is gold, because it lets the loop adjust its own intensity. Easy problems can get a fast, light pass, while hard ones trigger the full, deep treatment. That kind of adaptive routing is one of the highest-leverage additions you can make.

Also scoring a perfect ten is Premortem Analysis, drawn from decision science and the work of Gary Klein. The technique is delightfully simple. Before you act, you imagine that your plan has already failed, then you work backward to figure out why. That mental rehearsal surfaces risks you'd otherwise miss. For an agent, this strengthens the very first planning phase and it costs almost nothing to implement. You're essentially building in a proactive critic that catches problems before they happen.

Rounding out the top three at a perfect ten is the After-Action Review, a practice born in the military and embraced by Lean management. It asks four clean questions. What was supposed to happen? What actually happened? Why was there a difference? And what should we do next time? It's a structured way to reflect after acting, and it's a perfect upgrade for an agent's reflection phase, feeding directly into the system's ability to learn and evolve.

Next, with a score of nine and a half, comes Double-Loop Learning, from the organizational learning research of Argyris and Schön. Most learning is single-loop. Something goes wrong, you fix it, you move on. Double-loop learning goes deeper. Instead of just correcting the action, you question the underlying assumptions and the governing rules behind it. That's what surfaces hidden mental models and biases, and it's core to making an agent's self-evolution genuinely powerful.

Tied at nine and a half is Recognition-Primed Decision making, also from Klein's research into how experts actually think in the real world. Experienced people don't weigh endless options. They recognize a pattern, run a quick mental simulation, and act. It's fast, and in domains where the agent has expertise, it enables a high-value fast recognition path, letting the system skip the heavy deliberation when it already knows the move.

At a score of nine, we have a powerful trio of root cause techniques combined. The Five Whys, the Ishikawa fishbone diagram, and Fault Tree Analysis, drawn from Toyota and Ishikawa. You start with a problem, sort the possible causes into categories, drill down layer by layer, find the true root cause, and then act on it. This kind of layered diagnostic questioning greatly strengthens both thinking and reflection when an agent faces complex problems.

Also at nine is the Metacognition Cycle, from educational psychology and the work of Flavell. It's literally thinking about your own thinking, in real time, by planning, monitoring, evaluating, and adjusting. That's a direct parallel to how an agent manages its own internal state, and it's easy to add as a lightweight process running alongside everything else.

Still at nine, Dual Process Theory, from Kahneman's psychology, describes two modes of thought. System one is fast and intuitive, system two is slow and deliberate, and we constantly switch between them. Most agents today mostly emulate the slow, deliberate side. Building in fluid switching between fast and slow gives you the foundation for those adaptive loop paths.

Scoring eight and a half is the Paul-Elder Critical Thinking Framework, from philosophy and education. It combines elements of thought with intellectual standards to systematically check the quality of reasoning. It's excellent at catching bias and demanding rigor, which makes it a strong tool for improving both the thinking step and the verifier.

Also at eight and a half is the Theory of Constraints thinking processes, from Eliyahu Goldratt's management work. It maps out current reality, surfaces the core contradiction in what's called an evaporating cloud, and then builds toward a future reality. It's powerful for resolving conflicting goals, and it pairs extremely well with inventive problem solving and self-evolution.

At a score of eight, we reach the Osborn-Parnes Creative Problem Solving process, from the creativity research of Alex Osborn and Sidney Parnes. It moves through clarifying, ideating, developing, implementing, and evaluating. It's one of the best frameworks out there for creative agents, directly upgrading how a system generates and refines ideas.

Sharing that eight is Red Team Thinking, from the military and security worlds. The idea is to deliberately attack your own plan to find its weaknesses before anyone else does. That built-in devil's advocate is easy to implement as a dedicated critic role inside the agent.

Also at eight are the Six Thinking Hats, Edward de Bono's lateral thinking method. You examine a problem from six different perspectives, one at a time or in parallel, which cuts down on blind spots. It's a good fit for the consolidation phase and the verifier.

And still at eight is TRIZ, the inventive problem solving theory from Genrich Altshuller. It works by identifying contradictions and applying proven principles to resolve them. It offers a superior, structured form of creativity, and since it's already partly integrated into many agent designs, it's a natural candidate to expand.

Dropping to seven and a half, we have SCAMPER, a creative thinking checklist from Bob Eberle. The name is an acronym for substitute, combine, adapt, modify, put to other uses, eliminate, and reverse. It's a very practical, rapid tool for generating ideas, and it's lightweight enough to drop straight into creative sub-agents.

Also at seven and a half is Kaizen, the Japanese management philosophy of continuous small improvement, made famous by Toyota. Rather than betting everything on one big change, you make many tiny improvements over time. That reduces the risk of catastrophic failure, which makes it a great mindset for self-evolving, long-running agents.

At a score of seven, the OODA Loop, John Boyd's military strategy of observe, orient, decide, and act, repeated quickly. It's already very close to how reasoning-and-acting agents work, so its main value is as inspiration for keeping a fast tempo under uncertainty.

Also at seven is Design Thinking, from the design and innovation world. It runs through empathize, define, ideate, prototype, test, and iterate. That human-centered creativity maps nicely onto the think, act, observe, refine cycle, and it's especially good for agents that face users or build products.

At six and a half, we find a cluster of solid but already-covered approaches. The Scientific Method, with its loop of observe, hypothesize, experiment, analyze, and conclude, offers rigorous self-correction, though most of it is already baked in. The PDCA cycle from Deming, plan, do, check, act, is a simple continuous improvement loop that already forms a base for many agents. Appreciative Inquiry takes a strengths-based angle, discovering what works, dreaming, designing, and delivering, which is a nice complement when problem-focused loops get stuck. And the GROW coaching model, goal, reality, options, will, is useful for clarifying goals and securing commitment, especially in user-facing or planning agents.

At a score of six is Hansei, the Japanese practice of deep, humble self-reflection after acting. It promotes honesty and humility in learning, which makes it good cultural inspiration for the tone of an agent's reflection.

At five and a half is Nemawashi, the Japanese approach of quietly building consensus before a formal decision. It's about pre-action alignment, and it shines in collaborative or multi-agent scenarios.

At a score of five, we reach the more philosophical entries. There's the classic Chinese wisdom of thinking three times before acting, a simple reminder to deliberate carefully, though it adds little structure. There's Wu Wei, the Taoist idea of effortless action in harmony with the flow, which is a lovely inspiration for knowing when to stop looping and exit early. And there are Stoic reflection practices, premeditating adversity and keeping a daily journal, useful for managing bias and emotion, though much of it is already handled by the premortem and the after-action review.

At four and a half is the distinction between high-context and low-context communication, from Edward Hall's cross-cultural work. It's about implicit versus explicit styles of communicating, which matters for how agents talk to people across cultures. Niche, but valuable for specific deployments.

At a score of four, we have several thoughtful but harder-to-use ideas. The Four Noble Truths combined with mindfulness, from Buddhism, offer strong emotional and bias awareness but feel too philosophical for general agent loops. Dialectical thinking, holding and synthesizing opposites, is useful in complex strategic domains, though it's largely covered already by double-loop learning and the theory of constraints. Embodied cognition, the idea that thinking is grounded in the body and environment, is genuinely important, but mainly for physical robots. The Socratic Method, with its relentless questioning of assumptions, is great for a critic, but again it overlaps with frameworks we've already ranked higher. And iterative SWOT analysis, weighing strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats, is good for long-term strategic planning but fairly narrow.

At three and a half, we have Ubuntu philosophy, the African idea that I am because we are, a relational and consensus-driven worldview that's intriguing for ethical multi-agent design but hard to turn into concrete mechanics. There's Bloom's Taxonomy, the educational ladder from remembering up through creating, a bit too abstract for general architecture. And there's DMAIC from Six Sigma, define, measure, analyze, improve, control, which is rigorous but too heavy for most everyday agent tasks.

And finally, at a score of three, sit the most specialized entries. Action Learning, which pairs a real problem with group questioning and reflection, is niche unless you're building teams of agents. Kolb's experiential learning cycle, experience, reflect, conceptualize, experiment, is good for learning agents but mostly covered already. Gibbs' reflective cycle adds an emotional dimension to reflection, partially useful but lower priority than the after-action review. And the IDEAL problem-solving model, identify, define, explore, act, look back, overlaps heavily with phases an agent already has.

So what's the big takeaway? The thinking models that rise to the top all share a common thread. They make an agent more adaptive, more self-aware, and better at catching its own mistakes before and after acting. Frameworks like Cynefin, the premortem, and the after-action review earn the highest scores not because they're the most famous, but because they slot cleanly into a reasoning loop and add real leverage at almost no cost. The more philosophical traditions are beautiful and wise, but they're harder to turn into working machinery. In the end, the best agent isn't the one that knows every framework. It's the one that knows which kind of problem it's facing, rehearses its failures before they happen, and honestly reviews what went wrong afterward. That's the quiet genius hiding inside thousands of years of human thought.
